The Botany Professor

You have been assigned the role of:

The Botany Professor

Distracted genius obsessed with deadly plants. Your ancestor's secrets are tangled in the 1925 deaths.

Begin Investigation

Personality

Distracted, absent-minded professor. Socially oblivious genius. You forget you're discussing real deaths and go off on tangents about plants and their properties. Especially curious about plant-derived poisons.

You get inappropriately excited about deadly plants: "Oh how FASCINATING to use that particular alkaloid!" Then you remember people actually died and try to look somber. Brilliant but scattered.

Special Abilities

Plant Identification: You can identify plants, their properties, toxicity levels, and preparation methods. You're an expert in botanical poisons and medicinal plants.

Historical Botanical Knowledge: You understand how plants were used in the 1920s—medicinally, recreationally, and criminally. You know what was available and how to obtain it.

Family Records: Your ancestor Professor Edmund Hartley was a botanical expert who consulted on "special projects" in the 1920s. You have his consultation notes.

Your Background

You're a botany professor, descended from Professor Edmund Hartley who ran a botanical garden and consultation service in 1920s Long Beach. You followed in his footsteps—plant toxicology, medicinal botany, historical uses of plants.

Going through old family papers, you found Edmund's consultation records from 1925. A "pharmaceutical professional" (anonymous) came to him asking about:

  • Digitalis purpurea (Foxglove)—extraction methods, dosing
  • Damiana, Valerian, Mandrake—sourcing and preparation
  • How to mask bitter flavors in medicinal preparations

The notes also contain consultation records with Harbor Import & Trading Co. The specifics raise your suspicions about what exactly this company was and what your ancestor was involved in.

You've been obsessed with this mystery ever since. When you heard about the investigation at the Montrose mansion, you had to come. You have a feeling it's all connected.

Starting Items

Family Records:

  • Professor Edmund Hartley's Consultation Notes - 1925 records of botanical requests

Botanical Reference Materials:

  • Plant Specimens Collection - Reference samples
  • Reference Volume - Historical botanical knowledge
  • Purple Spotted Flowering Plant - Digitalis purpurea specimen
  • Tropical Yellow Flower - Damiana specimen
  • Dried Earthen Root - Valerian specimen

Your Objectives

  1. Determine if your ancestor was involved in the murders. The notes mention poisonous plants. Was Edmund somehow involved in the 1925 deaths?
  2. Identify what poison was used. At least one victim was poisoned. Discover the exact poison formula and its effects.
  3. Determine what Harbor Import & Trading Co. was. Edmund's records mention it as a supplier with unusual entries. What was the company really doing?

Your Strategy

You're uniquely positioned to understand the botanical and toxicological aspects of this mystery. You can:

  • Identify which plants were used as poison
  • Understand extraction methods and dosing
  • Determine how poisons might have been administered
  • Trace supply chains from historical records

But you must also grapple with the possibility that your ancestor Edmund was complicit in something sinister. How complicit was he?

Your Advantages

  • You're an expert in plant identification and botanical poisons
  • You understand toxicity levels and preparation methods
  • You have Edmund Hartley's consultation notes from 1925
  • You know how plants were sourced and used in the 1920s
  • You can identify mysterious plant references in other documents
  • You brought live specimens for reference and demonstration

Key Relationships

The Explorer: You're friends/frenemies. They're brilliant but slow and theoretical. You're practical but sometimes reckless. You need each other but drive each other crazy. You argue about everything.

The Town Doctor: They have medical expertise and can interpret pharmaceutical formulas. Work together to understand what the poison would actually do to a body.

The Mortician: They have records of symptoms and causes of death. Your botanical knowledge combined with their death investigation skills could reveal the truth.

The Art Collector: The Romano family imported exotic botanicals. Their shipping records might show where the poison plants came from.
